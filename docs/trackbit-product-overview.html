01 The problem

Schools don't fail on strategy. They fail on the days.

A school is a promise made 200 times a day, one period at a time. Whether that promise is kept — the class taught, the syllabus paced, the weak student caught, the parent informed — is almost impossible for a principal to actually see. The result is a school run on faith and hindsight.

The director is flying blind. What they know about today is a monthly summary, written after the fact and often reverse-engineered to look fine. Were all eight periods held? Is the syllabus actually on pace? They find out a class is eight chapters behind a week before the exam — when nothing can be done.

The teacher has no minute to spare. The same attendance and homework get written into a register, a diary, and a WhatsApp group. Any system that asks them to mark a 40-student class one by one is dead on arrival, so the data is never captured cleanly, and nothing downstream can be trusted.

The parent is the last to know. A call to the office, and still no clear answer about their child's day — what was taught, what homework was set, what was missed while the child was absent.

And the tools they bought died by March. Every one demanded data entry nobody had time for. The information lives scattered across Excel and WhatsApp, none of it connected, none of it queryable, no single source of truth.

The gap is never the plan. It is the distance between the plan and what actually happened — and no one can see across it in time to act.

02 The core idea

Capture by exception: confirm the norm, log only what changed.

Every hard problem above comes down to one thing — getting clean, daily ground truth without adding work. TrackBit's answer is a single mechanism that runs through the whole product: the teacher confirms the normal case in one tap, and records only the deviations.

Attendance is "all present," minus the few you tap. The daily plan is "we did the lesson," minus what didn't get done. A period goes on the record in four taps, under thirty seconds. Because the teacher gets value the instant they give data — homework logged is homework the parent already sees — the loop sustains itself. From that one honest tap, everything else is computed, not entered: per-student growth, syllabus pace, the parent's view, the director's dashboard, and the report that writes itself.

03–11 The modules, in depth

One loop, ten ways it pays off.

Each module below stands on its own, but every one reads from and writes to the same daily truth — so nothing is ever entered twice, and every screen agrees with every other.

03 · Academic planner & tracker

The whole year, planned down to the period — and held to it.

You give TrackBit the syllabus and the school calendar; it compiles a period-by-period plan for every class-subject, aware of terms, holidays and real teaching days. Chapters are sized against the actual periods available, so an over-ambitious portion is flagged before the year starts, not after.

Then it holds the school to that plan. The approved plan is the baseline; the classroom log is the actual. A live red / amber / green forecast shows exactly which class-subjects are on pace and which are slipping — and an exam-fit check answers the question that matters: can this portion still be finished before the test?

Term-scoped chapters Real teaching-day math RAG pace forecast Exam-fit check Mid-year adoption
04 · Timetable

Every teacher's week, mapped, clash-checked, and live.

The timetable maps periods to classes and teachers, with a deterministic clash detector that refuses to double-book a teacher. Import it from a photo or a spreadsheet and the system reads it in; from that grid, each teacher's day assembles itself into the list of periods they'll actually teach.

It is effective-dated and append-only, so a mid-term change to the schedule never rewrites history — last month's records still reflect the timetable that was truly in force then.

Photo / spreadsheet import Teacher clash detection Effective-dated changes
05 · Classroom logs & attendance

What happened in every classroom, captured in a single tap.

This is where capture by exception earns its keep. A teacher opens the period, taps "all present" and marks the few absentees, picks the topic from the syllabus list, and sets homework — the whole period, on the record, in seconds. An optional deep log lets the teacher flag only the students who stood out, never the whole class.

The first attendance of the day quietly triggers what the school needs it to — the record a parent will later see, the absence a class teacher should follow up. Nobody fills a register twice.

One-tap "all present" Topic from the plan Exception-only deep log "Not held" reasons
06 · Homework

Homework that reaches the parent the moment it's set.

Homework is logged as part of closing the period — class-wide, or for specific students when the work is personal. Because it lands in the same system the parent can see, the teacher gets value the instant they record it: the parent is informed without a single extra message being written.

Replaces: the homework diary, the class WhatsApp group, and the "my child forgot to write it down" phone call.

07 · Parent portal

Parents stop calling the office. They log in and see the day.

Every parent gets a login to their child's actual day: the topic taught in each period, the homework set, and — when their child was absent — exactly which topics they missed, so the gap can be closed at home. It is the class record, in the open, and it turns the school's daily capture into the parent's transparency at no extra effort.

What parents see is the record of the class and their child's part in it. The private intervention tiers that staff use to target help are never shown to a parent — transparency where it builds trust, discretion where it protects the child.

Per-period topics Homework set Missed-while-absent Performance over time
08 · Exams & student growth

Every child's growth, subject by subject, on one screen.

Enter exam results — by typing, or by dropping a photo of the marks sheet, which the system reads and pre-fills for review. Those scores flow into a growth view for every child: how much of each subject has actually been taught, the topics they missed while absent, their test trend over time, a skill profile, and the areas where they need help.

Behind the scenes, staff can group students into private A/B/C support tiers and spin up targeted interventions that become trackable tasks — the mechanism that turns "this child is slipping" into something the school actually does.

09 · The daily report

Nobody writes the report. It writes itself by 8 AM.

Overnight, TrackBit reads the whole day — attendance, topics covered, homework, syllabus pace, sessions and fees — and composes the report a principal would otherwise spend an hour assembling: the risks that need attention, the loose ends and ambiguities, and the wins worth celebrating. It is on the director's phone before the first bell.

Replaces: the Monday review deck, the WhatsApp round-up, and the hours spent chasing staff for numbers.

10 · Tasks & the director's board

The school's other work — on boards anyone can pick up.

Sitting on top of the academic core is a full task layer for everything else a school runs on: maintenance, housekeeping, events, admin. Work can be assigned to a person or left claimable on a public board; recurring routines materialise themselves. Crucially, every alert on the director's dashboard becomes a task in one tap — so a red syllabus forecast or a repeat absentee doesn't just get noticed, it gets owned.

Assign or claim Recurring routines Alert → task in one tap Full audit trail
11 · Fees & the hostel

Fees you can trust — and a day that doesn't end at the last bell.

The fee module handles structures, instalment plans, discounts and receipts on an append-only ledger — every payment, undo and adjustment is a new entry, never a silent edit, so the books can always be trusted. Fees are director-only; teachers never see a rupee.

For residential schools, the hostel runs on the same rails as the school day: evening prep, study blocks and activities, with rosters that compute themselves from class and boarder status. The child's day is tracked from the first bell to lights-out, in one continuous record.

12 The intelligence layer

Not a chatbot bolted on. A system that rebuilds itself around you.

Most "AI in edtech" is a chat window stapled to the side of an old product. TrackBit's AI is the opposite: it reaches into the live school through the same permissions a human has, and it can compose the interface itself. This is the part of the product with no equivalent in the market — and the reason a school that adopts TrackBit keeps discovering new value long after go-live.

The adaptive school OS

Ask it. And watch it build.

Underneath sits a registry of the school's real capabilities — every service the product exposes, described in a machine-readable way. The AI plans over those capabilities to answer a question or take an action, and then renders the answer as live interface: a table, a chart, a roster, a report card — chosen and assembled on the spot from real data it is not allowed to invent.

Shipped · agentic assistant

Lucy — ask your school anything.

"Which classes are behind on Science before the half-yearly?" Lucy reads the live plan and answers with a real, data-backed view — through the asker's own permissions, so a teacher sees their classes and a director sees the school. Anything that changes data waits for a human's confirmation.

Generative UI

Describe a view; it appears.

The interface is not a fixed set of screens. The AI materialises the right view from the data on demand — the numbers are always server-computed and never typed by the model. The system shows you what you asked to see, built in the moment.

describe → plan over capabilities → act (with confirm) → generate the interface   The same registry that powers this today is the seed of tomorrow: as the capability set grows, so does what can be described into being — from a new view, toward a new module.

A school that describes what it wants to track, and watches the module take shape. That is the direction we are building — a school operating system that reshapes itself to how each school actually works.

Everywhere else in the product, the same doctrine holds: AI proposes, a human confirms, and deterministic rules decide. It drafts the year plan, splits a chapter into teachable topics, reads a timetable photo, and writes the daily report — but every output lands in a review surface a person signs off before anything is saved. The intelligence makes the work effortless without ever putting the school on autopilot.

13 How it all connects

Why it survives past March, when other tools are abandoned.

The reason isn't a feature — it's the shape of the whole system. Value comes before data entry, and data entry is never a separate job.

Teachers give the least, and get the most. The only thing asked of a teacher is a few taps per period — and even that pays them back immediately, because logging homework informs the parent and closing a period clears their list. There is no data-entry role to hire, no clerk transcribing registers, no month-end scramble.

Everyone downstream reads one truth. The parent's portal, the director's dashboard, the growth tracker and the daily report are all views of the same captured day. Nothing is stored twice, so nothing can disagree. Fix a fact once and every screen updates.

It meets the school where it is. A school can adopt TrackBit mid-year, size chapters as each term begins, and run two campuses or a hostel — the system bends to that instead of demanding the year start over. Onboarding is done for the school, from the files it already has.

And it compounds. Every day of capture makes the growth history richer, the forecasts sharper, and the AI's answers more grounded. The product a school uses in year two is measurably more valuable than the one they started with — without a single upgrade to buy.

14 Trust & architecture

Built so the data can be trusted — and the private stays private.

Tenant isolation, enforced at the database.

Every school's data is scoped by a verified identity on every request and fenced by row-level security in the database itself — so a query can never reach across schools, even by mistake. Multi-school operation is a first-class design, not a bolt-on.

Append-only history — undo, never overwrite.

Fees, plan approvals, band changes and score verifications are recorded as new entries, not silent edits. An undo is a compensating entry. The books and the record can always be audited, and nothing quietly disappears.

Bands are private by design.

The A/B/C support tiers exist only to target help. They are staff-only and, as a hard product rule, never appear on any parent-facing surface. Discretion is enforced in the architecture, not left to policy.

AI that can't fabricate.

The assistant renders only server-computed data, and any action it proposes is a confirmable pending step, not a silent write. The model chooses how to present the truth; it is structurally prevented from inventing it.
